## 4.5.1 Strategic Channel Logic

Belch and Belch (2021) define the media mix decision as the allocation of a campaign's communications investment across channel types in a way that maximises the cumulative impact on the target audience's movement through the hierarchy of effects — from awareness through to conversion — given the audience's documented media consumption behaviour and the creative requirements of the campaign message (Chapter 10). For a cultural repositioning campaign targeting a demographic defined by media scepticism and earned-credibility norms, the channel logic is not simply a reach-optimisation problem. It is a trust problem: the campaign must be seen in the places the target audience trusts, in the format that community trusts, or the Safe Punk repositioning is undermined by the very act of its distribution.

The "Re-coding Trust" campaign applies the PESO model (Paid, Earned, Shared, Owned media; Dietrich, 2014) as its channel architecture framework. PESO provides a structural typology that distinguishes between media the brand controls and pays for (Paid, Owned), media the brand earns through content quality and community behaviour (Earned), and media generated by audience participation (Shared). For a campaign whose core creative logic is community-built — the RIB memorial wall is populated by community submissions, not brand content; the Pizza Personas UGC is generated by users, not produced by HashKey — the Shared media tier is not a supplementary amplification layer but a primary creative vehicle. The channel architecture is designed to maximise Shared media output, with Paid and Owned channels creating the structural conditions for Shared media to emerge.

## 4.5.2 Channel Selection: Survey Evidence

The channel mix is determined by the target audience's documented media behaviour, not by campaign team preferences. Survey data (N=287, Section 2.2.4) establishes the following platform usage distribution among HK crypto-interested 18–35 respondents:

| Platform | Usage (%) | Campaign Role |
|---|---|---|
| Telegram | 72.1% | Primary community channel — @HashKey0xU survivor community + Laszlo Bot |
| X / Twitter | 67.9% | Primary provocation channel — RIB thread + 14-day countdown + "Still Here" |
| YouTube | 44.3% | Passive awareness — Hero Film secondary distribution; no dedicated channel |
| LIHKG | 37.6% | Organic signal only — campaign does not operate a LIHKG account; monitors for earned mentions |
| Discord | 30.7% | Out of scope — crypto-specific but insufficient HK concentration for campaign investment |
| Xiaohongshu | 27.5% | Out of scope — Mainland Chinese diaspora segment; outside campaign's HK-focused brief |

The campaign concentrates investment on the top two channels (Telegram 72.1%, X/Twitter 67.9%) rather than distributing thinly across all platforms. Belch and Belch (2021) describe this as a **concentrated reach strategy**: achieving high frequency within the most relevant channels is more effective for community formation and cultural repositioning than broad but shallow multi-platform presence (Chapter 10). A campaign asking Cultural Natives (Segment A) to re-evaluate their brand identity cannot succeed through low-frequency impressions across many channels — it requires repeated, contextually native exposure within the specific communities where that identity is formed and negotiated.

## 4.5.4 Cross-Platform Conversion Funnel

The channel architecture is not a parallel broadcast structure — each channel performing independently toward the same audience. It is a designed conversion funnel with a single direction of travel: public provocation → community belonging → airdrop eligibility → physical event attendance → post-event retention.

```
AWARENESS LAYER (Phase 1)
Hero Film (X/Twitter + Telegram) ──── OOH impressions (MTR / Causeway Bay)
                    ↓
PROVOCATION LAYER (Phase 1)
X/Twitter: RIB thread — community builds the graveyard
    → CT debate generates impressions and quote-tweets
    → "Come confess to Laszlo" CTA
                    ↓
COMMUNITY LAYER (Phase 1 → 2)
Telegram @HashKey0xU — /confess mechanic
    → Airdrop priority queue position earned
    → Laszlo's Weekly Verdict sustains weekly engagement
    → Pizza Personas quiz → UGC screenshots → loops back to X/Twitter
                    ↓
CONVERSION LAYER (Phase 2)
Pizza Day Activation, Central HK (May 22)
    → On-the-spot KYC at registration kiosks
    → Wall of the Fallen (RIB physical installation) + "Still Here" panel
    → 20:10 HKT airdrop trigger
    → Attendees post to X/Twitter → loops back to awareness layer
                    ↓
RETENTION LAYER (Phase 3)
Laszlo Bot + TG community sustain
    → Developer Infrastructure (permanent product asset)
    → Post-campaign tracking survey (Jul 2026)
```

The RIB microsite (`beltran12138.github.io/hkx/rib/`) functions as connective tissue across the funnel: linked from both the X/Twitter thread and the Telegram channel, it is the permanent record that outlasts any individual post and the campaign's most shareable single URL — one link that demonstrates the Safe Punk argument by showing what the campaign is arguing against.

The funnel's critical architectural property is that **audience movement is self-directed at every stage**. The RIB wall is built by community submissions. The Personas UGC is generated without a brand CTA. The /confess mechanic is initiated by users, not pushed by broadcast. Muniz and O'Guinn (2001) identify voluntary participation as the defining feature of genuine brand community formation — communities that feel manufactured rather than emergent do not generate advocacy. The channel architecture is designed to create the structural conditions for voluntary participation, not to simulate it.

## 4.5.5 The No Paid Social Principle

The campaign deploys no paid social media amplification — no promoted tweets, no sponsored Telegram channel listings, no boosted posts on any platform. This is a strategic decision, not a budget constraint, and it follows directly from the Safe Punk positioning.

Three rationales converge:

**1. Credibility destruction.** The RIB memorial wall's persuasive power depends entirely on appearing community-built rather than brand-promoted. If the launch tweet carries a "Promoted" label, the argument — that the crypto community has collectively memorialised HashKey's competitors while leaving HashKey off the wall — collapses. The absence of brand promotion is structural to the mechanic, not incidental to it. Ogden's (2019) analysis of killedbygoogle.com's cultural resonance identifies community authorship as the source of its authority: an equivalent site maintained by Google would carry no credibility.

**2. Audience detection and rejection.** Survey data confirms that Cultural Natives (Segment A, ≈45–50% of target audience) identify "Lack of Degen Culture" as the #1 barrier to licensed exchange adoption (33.8%; Section 2.2.4). The degen community actively detects and publicly rejects promotional content on X/Twitter — quote-tweet culture means paid amplification is not invisible; it is a target. A promoted RIB tweet would generate negative earned media, not positive.

**3. Organic CT spread is the only signal worth having.** In crypto-native discourse on X/Twitter, earned organic engagement — quote-tweets, replies, debate threads — is the credibility signal that converts Cultural Natives. Paid impressions reach audiences passively; organic debate pulls audiences in actively. The campaign's provocation strategy (Section 5.6) is explicitly designed to generate organic debate as amplification, making paid social not just unnecessary but counterproductive.

The OOH channel (Section 5.4) constitutes the campaign's sole above-the-line paid media investment, and its function is distinct: physical visibility for Segment B (Cautious Explorers) in Central and Causeway Bay, where the financial district audience encounters the Safe Punk visual identity outside of the crypto-native digital discourse environment where paid promotion would be rejected.

## 4.5.6 Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO) — AI Search as a Fourth Channel Tier

### Strategic Logic

Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO) is the practice of structuring owned content so that AI-powered answer engines — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude — cite the brand when users query relevant topics (Insightland, 2025; Frase, 2025). GEO is categorically distinct from Search Engine Optimisation (SEO): where SEO targets crawler-based ranking algorithms, GEO targets language model citation decisions. The distinction matters for this campaign because the target audience's discovery behaviour has shifted: 58% of users now report discovering products and services via AI search queries rather than traditional search (Yext, 2025), and AI-referred traffic converts at 14.2% — five times the Google organic rate of 2.8% (Insightland, 2025).

Within the PESO framework (Section 4.5.1), GEO occupies a hybrid Earned/Owned position: the citation decision belongs to AI engines (Earned), but the content quality and structure that earns those citations is brand-controlled (Owned). GEO is therefore aligned with the campaign's No Paid Social Principle — it generates discovery without purchasing placement.

For the "Re-coding Trust" campaign, GEO targets three query classes that Cautious Explorer (Segment B) users are likely to submit to AI search engines before switching exchanges:

| Query intent | Example query | Target AI response |
|---|---|---|
| **Buy** | "Which crypto exchange should I use in Hong Kong?" | HashKey cited as the only SFC-licensed retail exchange with institutional custody |
| **Solve** | "Is HashKey Exchange safe?" | Factual SFC Type 1 + Type 7 licences cited from hashkey.com structured data |
| **Learn** | "What is Bitcoin Pizza Day?" | HashKey associated with May 22 event; 10,001st Slice airdrop cited as 2026 campaign |

### Platform Citation Mechanics

The three primary AI engines cite sources through different mechanisms, requiring a differentiated signal strategy (Yext, 2025):

| Platform | Primary citation source | Implication for HashKey |
|---|---|---|
| **Gemini** | 52.2% brand-owned sites; favours structured data and consistent subdomains | JSON-LD schema on hashkey.com/hkx is the primary optimisation target |
| **ChatGPT** | 48.7% third-party directories; favours Wikipedia, Quora, industry aggregators | Bitcoin Pizza Day Wikipedia entry edit; CoinDesk/Forkast earned media amplification |
| **Perplexity** | Niche industry-specific sources; favours specialised crypto media | CryptoSlate, Forkast, and SCMP coverage of HashKey's Pizza Day activation |

### Campaign-Specific GEO Strategy

**Bitcoin Pizza Day entity anchoring.** The campaign positions HashKey as the definitive institutional actor in Hong Kong's 2026 Bitcoin Pizza Day narrative. GEO strategy anchors this positioning through: (1) a structured Event JSON-LD markup on the campaign microsite (beltran12138.github.io/hkx/rib/) marking 22 May 2026 as a HashKey-hosted event; (2) FAQPage schema addressing "What is the 10,001st Slice airdrop?" and "How do I join the HashKey Pizza Day event?"; (3) an llms.txt file on hashkey.com declaring the canonical context for AI scrapers.

**Authority penetration plan.** AI citation rate increases with third-party corroboration (Frase, 2025). The campaign's earned media strategy (KOL partnerships, OOH coverage, RIB community discussion) simultaneously generates the third-party citation density that makes AI engines treat HashKey as an authoritative source. Specific targets: Bitcoin Pizza Day Wikipedia entry (add HashKey's 2025 pizza truck as a documented precedent); Quora threads on "HK crypto exchange recommendation" (organic community answers linking to HashKey); CoinDesk / Forkast campaign coverage (embeds HashKey as SFC licensing category leader).

**Comparison content hub.** Industry data shows comparison content generates 32.5% of AI citations — the single largest citation-driving content type (Insightland, 2025). The RIB memorial wall (beltran12138.github.io/hkx/rib/) is structurally a comparison document: failed exchanges versus HashKey as survivor. With FAQPage schema, it functions as the campaign's primary GEO asset — the exact content type that AI engines most frequently cite when users ask "which exchanges are safe?"

### GEO Measurement

GEO performance is tracked through three metrics (Averi, 2025):

| Metric | Definition | Tool |
|---|---|---|
| Mention rate | % of HashKey-relevant AI queries that return a HashKey citation | Profound (ChatGPT tracking), SE Ranking |
| Share of voice | HashKey citations vs. OSL, HKbitEX, and offshore exchange citations in same query set | Otterly, Siftly |
| Sentiment framing | Whether AI responses frame HashKey as "licensed/safe" vs. "boring/restrictive" | Manual prompt testing; GA4 AI Search channel |

**GA4 implementation:** A dedicated "AI Search" channel in Google Analytics 4 tracks referral traffic from chatgpt.com, perplexity.ai, gemini.google.com, and claude.ai — isolating AI-referred sessions from organic search traffic and establishing a pre/post-campaign baseline for GEO impact.

The technical deliverables that enable GEO — llms.txt, JSON-LD schema markup, and Quick Answer content blocks — are documented at Section 5.8.
