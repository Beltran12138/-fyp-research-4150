## 4.4.1 Objectives Framework

Colley (1961) established the foundational principle of modern advertising measurement in DAGMAR (*Defining Advertising Goals for Measured Advertising Results*): advertising objectives must be stated as specific communications tasks — not sales tasks — defined against a measurable baseline, directed at a defined target audience, and within a fixed time period. The intervention of variables between any communications exposure and a purchase decision makes sales-based objectives an unreliable instrument for evaluating campaign performance. Communications objectives, by contrast, measure what the campaign itself can produce: movement along the spectrum from unawareness to awareness, comprehension, conviction, and action (Colley, 1961).

Lavidge and Steiner (1961) provide the structural logic for sequencing communications objectives. Their hierarchy of effects model — Awareness → Knowledge → Liking → Preference → Conviction → Purchase — maps the cognitive, affective, and conative stages through which a consumer moves from ignorance of a brand to purchasing behaviour (pp. 59–62). The model implies that different campaign phases must serve different objective types: reach and awareness objectives cannot simultaneously function as conversion objectives, because they operate at different hierarchy levels and require different creative strategies. Belch and Belch (2021) operationalise this distinction within an IMC framework, recommending that campaign planning privilege communications objectives because they measure what the communications programme itself produces, not the downstream commercial outcomes that multiple non-campaign variables also influence (Chapter 7).

The objectives in this section apply these frameworks across two layers. The **primary objective** — the strategic mandate against which all campaign decisions are ultimately evaluated — is expressed as a brand health metric derived directly from the survey baseline (Section 2.2.4). The **secondary objectives** are organised across three hierarchy levels (awareness, engagement, conversion) and written to SMART criteria: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound (Doran, 1981). All objectives are benchmarked against survey data established in Section 2.2.4, and all are measurable through the framework detailed in Section 9.

## 4.4.2 Primary Objective: Closing the Trust-Engagement Gap

The campaign's primary objective is quantified, derived directly from the survey data, and expressed as a brand health target to be measured by a post-campaign tracking survey administered in July 2026.

**Primary Objective:** Reduce the Trust-Engagement Gap (Δ) from the survey baseline of Δ=2.52 (*t*(286)=18.73, *p*<.001; Section 2.2.4) to Δ≤1.75 by 31 July 2026, by increasing Brand Relatability to M≥3.50/7 while maintaining Asset Security Trust at M≥5.00/7.

The objective has two components that must be achieved simultaneously, not traded off against each other:

| Component | Baseline (Feb 2026) | Target (Jul 2026) | Movement Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brand Relatability | M = 2.75 / 7 | M ≥ 3.50 / 7 | +0.75 points (+27.3%) |
| Asset Security Trust | M = 5.27 / 7 | M ≥ 5.00 / 7 | No regression (floor) |
| Trust-Engagement Gap (Δ) | 2.52 points | ≤ 1.75 points | −0.77 reduction |

The asymmetry is intentional and theoretically grounded. Keller (2013) argues that brand knowledge structures consist of two independently manageable components — brand awareness (salience) and brand image (associations) — and that repositioning campaigns need not move both simultaneously (Chapters 8–9). HashKey's trust associations are already near-institutional (M=5.27/7). The campaign's task is to move the relatability score by a specific, measurable quantum without eroding the trust floor.

A +0.75 point movement on a 7-point Likert scale within a five-month campaign is justified by two evidence layers: (1) 50.2% of respondents indicated the "Safe Punk" concept framing would "likely" or "definitely" change their brand perception before any production had occurred (Section 2.2.4); (2) the Δ=2.52 gap is driven by cultural unfamiliarity, not product deficiency — the survey identifies "Lack of Degen Culture" (33.8%), not product failures, as the primary adoption barrier (Section 2.2.4). A communications-addressable problem requires a communications solution (Colley, 1961).

The secondary objective most directly operationalising this primary target is the reduction of "Boring" word associations: from the survey baseline of 39.4% (Section 2.2.4) to below 25% by July 2026 — a reduction of more than one-third. "Boring" is the single association most antagonistic to relatability, and its reduction is the clearest indicator that the "Safe Punk" repositioning is penetrating the target demographic's mental model of the brand.

## 4.4.3 Awareness Objectives

Awareness objectives operate at the cognitive tier of Lavidge and Steiner's (1961) hierarchy. They measure whether the campaign has achieved basic reach and message registration — whether target audience members know the campaign exists, can recall HashKey in connection with Bitcoin Pizza Day, and have encountered the "Safe Punk" concept. Awareness is the prerequisite for all downstream objectives.

**Objective A1 — Campaign Reach:** Achieve a minimum of 500,000 unique impressions across Telegram and X/Twitter combined within Phase 1 (1 February – 21 May 2026), targeting Hong Kong-based accounts within the 18–35 demographic, as measured by platform analytics and KOL post reach data.

*Rationale:* The target demographic's primary channels are Telegram (72.1%) and X/Twitter (67.9%; Section 2.2.4). Reach of 500,000 unique impressions represents approximately five exposures per member of the estimated 100,000-strong HK crypto-engaged 18–35 population (derived from SFC and industry sizing), consistent with minimum effective frequency thresholds in IMC planning literature (Belch & Belch, 2021, Chapter 10).

**Objective A2 — HashKey × Pizza Day Brand Association:** Achieve 40% unaided brand-to-Pizza Day association among HK crypto users aged 18–35 by 22 May 2026, as measured by a mid-campaign pulse survey (N≥100, same screening criteria as Section 2.2.2).

*Rationale:* The survey baseline shows near-zero HashKey × Pizza Day association among the sample. The 40% target is benchmarked against the existing 66.2% general Pizza Day awareness (Section 2.2.4): achieving 40% brand-to-event linkage from a cultural hook already reaching 66.2% of the audience represents a reasonable conversion rate from existing cultural familiarity to brand-specific association.

**Objective A3 — "Safe Punk" Concept Recognition:** Achieve 50% awareness of the "Safe Punk" concept framing (unaided or aided) among the 18–24 primary cohort by 31 May 2026, as measured by the post-campaign tracking survey.

*Rationale:* Pre-campaign concept testing found 56.3% positive receptivity among 18–24 respondents (Section 2.2.4) — a population with no prior campaign exposure. With active Phase 1 media presence across OOH, social, and KOL channels, 50% awareness among the primary cohort is a conservative and achievable target.

## 4.4.4 Engagement Objectives

Engagement objectives operate at the affective tier. They measure whether the campaign has moved beyond passive awareness into active participation — whether target audience members are interacting with campaign content, self-selecting into community mechanics, and demonstrating the cultural affinity that precedes conversion. Schultz and Schultz (2004) argue that the next generation of IMC must privilege behavioural evidence over attitudinal self-report; engagement metrics — shares, UGC volume, community join-rates — are the closest behavioural proxy for attitudinal shift in the pre-conversion phase.

**Objective E1 — 0xU Community Membership:** Achieve 5,000 members in the @HashKey0xU Institutional Degen Union Telegram channel by 22 May 2026, with a minimum of 40% demonstrating active engagement (message reactions, forwards, or replies within any 30-day window).

*Rationale:* The @HashKey0xU community channel is the primary Phase 1 brand narrative vehicle. 5,000 members within a focused HK crypto demographic constitutes a high-density, high-engagement community consistent with Telegram's architecture as a peer-to-peer trust channel (Section 2.2.4). The 40% active engagement floor distinguishes community quality from passive subscriber accumulation.

**Objective E2 — Pizza Persona UGC Generation:** Generate a minimum of 2,000 pieces of user-generated content featuring Pizza Personas (Telegram sticker forwards, X/Twitter posts, and screenshot shares) between Phase 1 launch and 31 May 2026.

*Rationale:* UGC serves a dual function: it demonstrates genuine cultural adoption of the "Safe Punk" creative frame, and it generates organic peer-to-peer amplification that cannot be purchased. The SBTI-informed quiz mechanic (Section 5.5) is designed for screenshot sharing as the primary social action, lowering the UGC creation threshold to a single tap. 2,000 UGC pieces across a five-month Phase 1 window — approximately 400 per month — sets a quality threshold rather than a volume one.

**Objective E3 — Laszlo Bot Interaction Sessions:** Achieve 15,000 unique user interaction sessions with the Laszlo Bot (Section 5.1) across Telegram, X/Twitter, and hashkey.com between Phase 1 launch and 31 May 2026, with a minimum average interaction depth of 3 exchanges per session.

*Rationale:* Bot engagement depth (average exchanges per session) is a stronger indicator of content resonance than raw session volume. A 3-exchange minimum filters for users who found the interaction substantive enough to continue. 15,000 unique sessions at this depth threshold represents meaningful cultural engagement relative to the target community size.

## 4.4.5 Conversion Objectives

Conversion objectives operate at the conative tier — the point at which attitudinal change produces measurable behaviour. Colley (1961) distinguishes between "action" as the final stage of the communications spectrum and sales as a downstream commercial outcome. Campaign conversion objectives should measure action (account registration, airdrop participation), not revenue, because multiple variables beyond the campaign influence revenue outcomes.

**Objective C1 — Airdrop Participant Registration:** Achieve 10,001 verified and KYC-completed HashKey account holders eligible to participate in the "10,001st Slice" airdrop by 22 May 2026 at the BTC price trigger event.

*Rationale:* The 10,001-person threshold is simultaneously a campaign creative decision (Section 4.2) and a measurable conversion target. The cap transforms the airdrop from a mass-distribution event into a cultural threshold: being among the 10,001 is a statement of timing, discernment, and belonging. Allen, Berg, and Lane (2023) identify the dual function of airdrops as both marketing acquisition tools and community decentralisation signals; the "10,001st Slice" mechanic exploits both functions simultaneously. The KYC-completion requirement ensures this metric measures genuine platform adoption, not speculative wallet farming.

**Objective C2 — Net-New Account Registrations:** Generate a minimum of 3,000 net-new KYC-completed HashKey account registrations attributable to campaign activity (tracked via campaign UTM links, QR codes at OOH placements and the Pizza Day event, and referral codes) between 1 February and 30 June 2026.

*Rationale:* Exclusive token airdrop is the preferred conversion incentive for 45.6% of the target demographic, and physical pizza event for 18.1% — together, these two campaign mechanics directly address 63.7% of the audience's preferred action triggers (Section 2.2.4). 3,000 new registrations across a five-month campaign window represents an average of 600 per month, benchmarked against industry norms for licensed exchange new-user growth during event-driven campaigns.

**Objective C3 — Post-Campaign Retention:** Achieve a minimum of 40% 30-day active trading rate among campaign-acquired accounts (registered during the campaign period), measured 30 days after Pizza Day activation (by 22 June 2026).

*Rationale:* Acquisition without retention produces no durable brand value. Schultz and Schultz (2004) argue that the measurement of IMC returns must extend beyond the initial action to customer retention behaviour. A 40% 30-day retention rate is the minimum threshold at which campaign-acquired users can be considered genuinely converted, rather than airdrop farmers who register for the drop and disengage. This metric also functions as an early signal for Phase 3 community sustain strategy (Section 4.1.3).

The thirteen objectives are organised in descending priority: brand health objectives (P1–P4) constitute the primary mandate; awareness objectives (A1–A3) are the reach preconditions; engagement objectives (E1–E3) are the cultural penetration signals; conversion objectives (C1–C3) are the behavioural outcomes. Where objectives conflict — for example, if maximising reach (A1) requires broadening targeting beyond the core 18–24 cohort — brand health objectives take precedence, because a campaign that achieves reach without closing the Δ=2.52 gap has not fulfilled its strategic mandate.
