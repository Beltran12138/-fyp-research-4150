## 4.3.1 Objectives Framework

Colley (1961) established the foundational principle of modern advertising measurement in DAGMAR (*Defining Advertising Goals for Measured Advertising Results*): that advertising objectives must be stated as specific communications tasks — not sales tasks — defined against a measurable baseline, directed at a defined target audience, within a fixed time period. The intervention of any number of variables between a communications exposure and a purchase decision makes sales-based objectives an unreliable instrument for evaluating communications effectiveness. Communications objectives, by contrast, measure what communications can actually produce: movement along the spectrum from unawareness to awareness, comprehension, conviction, and action.

Lavidge and Steiner (1961) provide the structural logic for how communications objectives should be sequenced. Their hierarchy of effects model — Awareness → Knowledge → Liking → Preference → Conviction → Purchase — maps the cognitive, affective, and conative stages through which a consumer moves from ignorance of a brand to purchase behaviour (pp. 59–62). The model implies that different campaign phases must serve different objective types: reach-and-awareness objectives cannot simultaneously function as conversion objectives, because they operate at different hierarchy levels and require different creative strategies and channels. Belch and Belch (2021) operationalise this distinction within an IMC framework, differentiating between communications-oriented objectives (awareness, attitude, association) and sales-oriented objectives, and recommending that campaign planning privilege communications objectives because they measure what the communications programme itself produces (Chapter 7).

The objectives in this section apply these frameworks in two layers. The **primary objective** — the strategic mandate against which all campaign decisions are ultimately evaluated — is expressed as a brand health metric derived from the survey baseline (Section 2.2.1). The **secondary objectives** are organised across three hierarchy levels (awareness, engagement, conversion) and written to SMART criteria: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound (Doran, 1981, as adapted for marketing communications). All objectives are benchmarked against the survey data established in Sections 2.2.1 and 3.3, and are measurable through the KPI framework detailed in Section 4.7.

## 4.3.2 Primary Objective: Closing the Trust-Engagement Gap

The campaign's primary objective is quantified, derived directly from the survey data, and expressed as a brand health target to be measured by a post-campaign tracking survey administered in July 2026.

**Primary Objective:** Reduce the Trust-Engagement Gap (Δ) from the survey baseline of Δ=2.52 (Trust M=5.27/7; Relatability M=2.75/7; *t*(286)=24.41, *p*<.001; Section 2.2.1) to Δ≤1.75 by 31 July 2026, by increasing Brand Relatability to M≥3.50/7 while maintaining Asset Security Trust at M≥5.00/7.

The objective has two components that must be achieved simultaneously, not traded off against each other:

| Component | Baseline (Feb 2026) | Target (Jul 2026) | Movement Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brand Relatability (Q11b) | M = 2.75 / 7 | M ≥ 3.50 / 7 | +0.75 points (+27.3%) |
| Asset Security Trust (Q11a) | M = 5.27 / 7 | M ≥ 5.00 / 7 | No regression (floor) |
| Trust-Engagement Gap (Δ) | 2.52 points | ≤ 1.75 points | −0.77 reduction |

The asymmetry is intentional and theoretically grounded. Keller (2013) argues that brand knowledge structures consist of two independent components — brand awareness (salience) and brand image (associations) — and that these can be managed independently (Chapters 8–9). HashKey's awareness and trust associations are already strong. The campaign does not need to move the trust score; it needs to move the relatability score by a specific, measurable quantum. A +0.75 point movement on a 7-point Likert scale, within a five-month campaign period, is justified by two evidence layers: (1) 49.1% of respondents indicated the "Safe Punk" concept framing would "likely" or "definitely" change their brand perception before any production had begun (Section 3.3.6); (2) the Δ=2.52 gap is driven primarily by cultural unfamiliarity, not product deficiency — a communications-addressable problem (Colley, 1961). A product shortfall would require a different intervention.

The secondary objective that most directly operationalises this primary target is the reduction of "Boring" word associations: from the survey baseline of 37.6% (Section 3.3.5) to below 25% by July 2026, a reduction of more than one-third. "Boring" is the single word association most directly antagonistic to relatability, and its reduction is the clearest indicator that the "Safe Punk" cultural repositioning is penetrating the target audience's mental model of the brand.

## 4.3.3 Awareness Objectives

Awareness objectives operate at the cognitive tier of Lavidge and Steiner's (1961) hierarchy. They measure whether the campaign has achieved basic reach and message registration — whether target audience members know the campaign exists, can recall HashKey in connection with Bitcoin Pizza Day, and have encountered the "Safe Punk" concept. Awareness is a prerequisite for all downstream objectives: no engagement or conversion is possible without prior awareness.

**Objective A1 — Campaign Reach:** Achieve a minimum of 500,000 unique impressions across Telegram and Twitter/X combined within Phase 1 (1 February–21 May 2026), targeting Hong Kong-based accounts within the 18–35 demographic, as measured by platform analytics and KOL post reach data.

*Rationale:* The target demographic's primary channels are Telegram (72.1%) and Twitter/X (67.9%; Section 3.3.2). Reach of 500,000 unique impressions represents approximately five exposures per member of the estimated 100,000-person HK crypto-engaged 18–35 population (derived from SFC and industry sizing; Section 3.2), consistent with the minimum effective frequency threshold identified in IMC planning literature (Belch & Belch, 2021, Chapter 10).

**Objective A2 — HashKey × Pizza Day Brand Association:** Achieve 40% unaided brand-to-Pizza Day association among HK crypto users aged 18–35 by 22 May 2026, as measured by a mid-campaign pulse survey (N≥100, same screening criteria as Section 2.2.1).

*Rationale:* The survey baseline shows near-zero HashKey × Pizza Day association (HashKey activated Pizza Day in 2025 but without a sustained campaign narrative). The 40% target is benchmarked against the existing 63.1% general Pizza Day awareness (Section 3.3.6): achieving 40% brand-to-event linkage from a cultural hook that already reaches 63.1% of the audience represents a reasonable conversion rate from existing cultural awareness to brand association.

**Objective A3 — "Safe Punk" Concept Recognition:** Achieve 50% awareness of the "Safe Punk" concept framing (unaided or aided) among the 18–24 primary cohort by 31 May 2026, as measured by the post-campaign tracking survey.

*Rationale:* Pre-campaign concept testing found 56.3% positive receptivity among 18–24 respondents (Section 3.3.6) — a population that had not yet been exposed to any campaign execution. With active media investment across Phase 1, 50% awareness among the primary cohort is a conservative and achievable target.

## 4.3.4 Engagement Objectives

Engagement objectives operate at the affective tier of the hierarchy. They measure whether the campaign has moved beyond passive awareness into active participation — whether target audience members are interacting with campaign content, self-selecting into community mechanics, and demonstrating the cultural affinity that precedes conversion. Schultz and Schultz (2004) argue that the next generation of IMC must privilege behavioural evidence over attitudinal self-report; engagement metrics — shares, UGC, community join-rates — are the closest behavioural proxy for attitudinal shift in the pre-conversion phase.

**Objective E1 — 0xU Community Membership:** Achieve 5,000 members in the 0xU Institutional Degen Union Telegram channel by 22 May 2026 (Pizza Day activation), with a minimum of 40% demonstrating active engagement (message reactions, forwards, or replies within any 30-day window).

*Rationale:* The 0xU community channel is the primary Phase 1 brand narrative vehicle. 5,000 members within a focused HK crypto demographic represents a high-density, high-engagement community rather than a mass audience — consistent with Telegram's architecture as a peer-to-peer trust channel (Section 3.3.2). The 40% active engagement floor distinguishes community quality from passive subscriber accumulation.

**Objective E2 — Pizza Persona UGC Generation:** Generate a minimum of 2,000 pieces of user-generated content featuring Pizza Personas (Telegram sticker forwards, Twitter/X posts, LIHKG thread mentions) between Phase 1 launch and 31 May 2026.

*Rationale:* UGC serves a dual function: it demonstrates genuine cultural adoption of the "Safe Punk" creative frame, and it generates organic peer-to-peer amplification that cannot be purchased. 2,000 UGC pieces across a five-month Phase 1 window — approximately 400 per month — is a modest target designed to set a quality threshold rather than a volume one. LIHKG inclusion is deliberate: 37.6% of the target audience uses LIHKG (Section 3.3.2), and authentic LIHKG discourse is the clearest signal of genuine community adoption among sceptical, culturally-critical respondents.

**Objective E3 — Safe Punk AI Persona (Coze Bot) Engagement:** Achieve 15,000 unique user interaction sessions with the Coze Bot across Telegram, Twitter/X, and the HashKey website between Phase 1 launch and 31 May 2026, with a minimum average interaction depth of 3 exchanges per session.

*Rationale:* Bot engagement depth (average exchanges per session) is a stronger indicator of content resonance than raw session volume. A 3-exchange minimum filters for users who found the interaction substantive enough to continue, as opposed to single-touch curiosity encounters. 15,000 unique sessions with this depth threshold represents a meaningful cultural engagement volume relative to the target community size.

## 4.3.5 Conversion Objectives

Conversion objectives operate at the conative tier of the hierarchy — the point at which attitudinal change produces measurable behaviour. For HashKey, the primary conversion is a new account registration (KYC-completed); the secondary conversion is campaign-attributable trading activity. Colley (1961) distinguishes between "action" as the final stage of the communications spectrum and sales as a downstream commercial outcome — campaign conversion objectives should measure action (account registration, airdrop participation), not revenue, because multiple factors beyond the campaign influence revenue.

**Objective C1 — Airdrop Participant Registration:** Achieve 10,001 verified and KYC-completed HashKey account holders eligible to participate in the "10,001st Slice" airdrop by 22 May 2026 at the BTC price trigger event.

*Rationale:* The 10,001-person threshold is simultaneously a campaign creative decision (Section 4.2.5) and a measurable conversion target. The cap creates urgency without being arbitrarily restrictive: HashKey's reported user base and the survey's 17.8% licensed exchange usage rate suggest the total addressable HK crypto population is sufficiently large to make 10,001 verified accounts a stretch target rather than a floor. The KYC-completion requirement ensures this metric measures genuine platform adoption, not speculative wallet farming.

**Objective C2 — New Account Registrations:** Generate a minimum of 3,000 net-new KYC-completed HashKey account registrations attributable to campaign activity (tracked via campaign UTM links, QR codes, and referral codes) between 1 February and 30 June 2026.

*Rationale:* Token airdrop is the preferred conversion incentive for 43.9% of the target demographic (Section 3.3.6), and physical pizza event for 18.1% — together, these two campaign mechanics directly address 62.0% of the audience's preferred action triggers. 3,000 new registrations across a five-month campaign window represents an average of 600 per month, which is benchmarked against industry norms for licensed exchange new-user growth during event-driven campaigns rather than against HashKey's baseline (which is not publicly disclosed).

**Objective C3 — Post-Campaign Retention:** Achieve a minimum of 40% 30-day active trading rate among campaign-acquired accounts (registered during the campaign period), measured 30 days after the Pizza Day activation (by 22 June 2026).

*Rationale:* Acquisition without retention produces no durable brand value. Schultz and Schultz (2004) argue that the measurement of IMC returns must extend beyond the initial action to customer retention behaviour. A 40% 30-day retention rate is the minimum threshold at which campaign-acquired users can be considered genuinely converted (rather than airdrop farmers who register for the drop and disengage). This metric also functions as an early signal for the post-campaign Phase 3 sustain strategy (Section 4.5).

The thirteen objectives are organised in descending priority: brand health objectives (P1–P4) are the primary mandate against which the campaign is ultimately judged; awareness objectives (A1–A3) are the reach preconditions; engagement objectives (E1–E3) are the cultural penetration signals; conversion objectives (C1–C3) are the behavioural outcomes. Where objectives conflict — for example, if maximising reach (A1) requires broadening creative targeting beyond the core 18–24 cohort — brand health objectives take precedence, because a campaign that achieves reach without closing the Δ=2.52 gap has not accomplished its strategic mandate.
