## 4.1.3 Campaign Architecture

Keller (2009) argues that brand meaning is not the product of any single communication act but of "the integration of information from all sources of brand contact" — that is, the cumulative, consistent expression of brand identity across multiple touchpoints over time (p. 141). Schultz and Schultz (2004) extend this logic into an IMC planning principle: an integrated campaign is not a collection of independent executions sharing a logo, but a designed system in which each component performs a distinct function and all components reinforce a single strategic intent. The architectural task is therefore to specify not only what each campaign element does, but how the elements interact — where one element creates the conditions for another, how audience movement flows between them, and what the system produces collectively that no individual element could produce alone.

The "Re-coding Trust" campaign is designed as a seven-pillar system governed by a single meta-positioning — **"Safe Punk"** — expressed through the campaign concept "The 10,001st Slice" (Section 4.2). The meta-positioning functions as the architectural hub: every execution must be derivable from it, legible within it, and mutually reinforcing toward the same strategic outcome — reducing the Trust-Engagement Gap from Δ=2.52 to Δ≤1.75 by July 2026 (Section 4.4; Survey data, N=287, *t*(286)=18.73, *p*<.001).

### Three-Phase Campaign Structure

The seven pillars deploy across three phases, each serving a distinct function in the audience conversion journey (Lavidge & Steiner, 1961):

| Phase | Period | Primary Objective | Pillars Active | Primary Segment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1: Narrative Seeding** | 1 Feb – 21 May 2026 | Awareness + cultural credibility establishment among Segment A (Cultural Natives); begin Segment B consideration | Film (launch anchor) → OOH (visibility) → Social/RIB (provocation) → TG Channel (community formation) → Personas quiz (identity engagement) → Laszlo Bot (daily presence) → Developer Infra (beta launch) | Segment A primary; Segment B secondary |
| **Phase 2: Pizza Day Peak** | 22 May 2026 | All-segment convergence; maximum conversion; airdrop trigger; physical community formation | All seven pillars simultaneously active; Physical Event is the day's focal point; airdrop triggers at 20:10 HKT BTC price threshold | Segments A + B simultaneously |
| **Phase 3: Community Sustain** | 23 May – Jun 2026 | Retention of campaign-acquired users; post-conversion community deepening; advocacy activation; measurement initiation | Laszlo Bot (retention content) + TG Channel (ongoing community) + Developer Infra (permanent product) | Segment B (conversion depth); Segment A (advocacy) |

### Cross-Pillar Conversion Logic

The pillars are sequenced to produce a single directed audience journey from awareness to advocacy. The designed flow is as follows:

```
Hero Film  →  OOH impressions
    ↓
Social / RIB thread (X/Twitter): provocation generates debate
    ↓
"Come confess to Laszlo" CTA  →  Telegram @HashKey0xU channel
    ↓
/confess mechanic  →  airdrop priority queue + community belonging
    ↓
Pizza Personas quiz  →  identity recognition + UGC sharing
    ↓
Laszlo Bot: compliance case via crypto history  →  KYC CTA
    ↓
Pizza Day Activation (May 22): physical convergence
    →  on-the-spot KYC  →  airdrop trigger at 20:10 HKT
    →  event content posted back to X/Twitter  →  loops to top
    ↓
Developer Infrastructure: permanent post-campaign ecosystem asset
```

The critical architectural feature of this flow is that HashKey does not assert its own compliance credentials at any stage. The RIB memorial wall builds the graveyard by community participation; the "Still Here" counter-thread allows the wall to make the argument by omission. The Personas mechanic embeds the compliance case inside an identity recognition mechanism the audience wants to share. The airdrop mechanic converts cultural engagement into registered accounts. At every stage, the audience moves itself — the campaign creates the conditions, not the conclusion (Muniz & O'Guinn, 2001).

### Segment–Phase–Pillar Alignment

The two target segments move through the campaign system at different speeds and via different pillar combinations, reflecting the distinct decision architectures documented in Section 4.3:

| Segment | Phase 1 Entry Point | Conversion Trigger | Phase 2 Action | Phase 3 Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A — Cultural Natives** | RIB thread (X/Twitter) via organic CT engagement | Pizza Persona quiz result + airdrop scarcity (FOMO) | Airdrop registration + event attendance + UGC posting | Organic advocacy; 0xU community co-creator |
| **B — Cautious Explorers** | OOH impressions + KOL-referred Telegram channel | Social proof from Segment A adoption + airdrop + fee rebate | On-the-spot KYC at event kiosks + airdrop participation | 30-day active trading; brand relatability shift |

Segment A's cultural credibility conversion in Phase 1 is a structural prerequisite for Segment B's decision confidence in Phase 2: Cautious Explorers observe Cultural Natives adopting the Safe Punk identity and participating in the Telegram community before committing — a sequencing logic consistent with Belch and Belch's (2021) differentiated targeting model, in which a concentrated Phase 1 focus on the most culturally demanding segment creates the social-proof conditions that lower Phase 2 conversion costs for the adjacent segment.
